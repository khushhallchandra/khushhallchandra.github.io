Data on CS Graduate School Admissions

January 4, 2015

Disclaimer(s)

It is important to note that GPA and GRE scores are not the most important components of your application. In my opinion, your letters of recommendation, research, and industry experience are far more important. GPA and GRE are often used as a filter by admissions committees. Once you're above a certain threshold, these scores don't really matter. Other than publication and internship counts, GPA and GRE scores are other ways to objectively compare yourself to others.

Note: Data older than 2012 may not reflect the current state of the applicant pool. Interest in computer science is at a record high. This will cause the admissions process to become far more competitive than in previous years.

Outline

Although this post refers to the computer science admissions processes, it may be relevant for similar fields such as electrical engineering and computation-heavy disciplines. The rest of this post are organized into several sections:

1. Stanford Admissions Process

Although the exact procedure varies per school, the process is more or less the same. At Stanford, the computer science admissions process goes like this:
  1. The admissions office will filter out a large number of applicants. This is usually done through GPA and/or GRE scores. These applicants are rejected and that is the end of the process for them. Applicants who pass this filter proceed to a more thorough review.
  2. The remaining applications are then sent to the admissions committee. The committee usually consists of professors, lecturers, and/or PhD students. The exact composition of the committee varies by school. At Stanford, your application is sent to three random committee members.
  3. Each committee member reads your application and assigns a score between 1 and 5 inclusive. The scores from the three committee members are averaged. If your average score is below a threshold, say 3.5 (varies on the applicant pool), then you are given a preliminary rejection.
  4. The same three committee members who read your application will be notified of this preliminary rejection. At this point, if a single committee member decides to vouch for you (i.e. strongly says you should be admitted), the preliminary rejection will be overturned and you will move into the next round. If you are given a preliminary rejection and no admissions committee member vouches for you, then you will unfortunately be rejected.
  5. For PhD applicants, the three (or sometimes five) committee members usually read your application and see if you are a good fit with potential advisors you listed in your statement of purpose. If you are a good match, they will then send your application to that professor who will then read your application and make the final decision. The professor who accepts you will usually be your preliminary advisor (some schools do lab rotations for first year PhD students).
  6. Applicants who met the average score threshold (3.5 in our example) are then sorted by their average score. Acceptances are then sent to the applicants with the best scores. Again, the exact score threshold or number of accepted students varies but I've provided data below.

5. GPA

In this section, I will assume the GPA is out of a 4.0 scale.

For top applicants, GPA is not a good metric to use for comparisons. Some students take graduate courses (and usually get As due to grade inflation in graduate courses) while some students take difficult, non-required undergraduate courses (e.g. real analysis) and hurt their GPA. Because of this, schools tend to use GPA as a filter. If your GPA is above 3.7 for example, spending tens (or hundreds?) of hours trying to improve this to a 3.75 or 3.8 will not help you in a significant manner. This becomes more true as your GPA approaches 3.9+.
